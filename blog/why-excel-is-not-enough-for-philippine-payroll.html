Why Excel Is Not Enough for Philippine Payroll

You're not bad at Excel. That's actually the problem.

← Back to blog

We're not going to tell you that you don't know how to use Excel. You probably do. You likely have a VLOOKUP or two, some nested IF formulas, a payroll template you spent a Saturday afternoon building. And it works. For a while.

That's the problem.

Excel works until it doesn't. And in Philippine payroll specifically, there are more ways to hit that wall than most business owners realize until they're already past it.

What payroll in the Philippines actually involves

Philippine payroll is not a simple multiplication. Every cutoff requires you to account for:

  • SSS contribution brackets — the table was updated in 2023 and the rate has been increasing every year since RA 11199 took effect
  • PhilHealth premiums — currently at 5% with an income ceiling of ₱100,000 per month
  • Pag-IBIG contributions — straightforward, but has a maximum monthly compensation base that needs to be capped
  • TRAIN Law withholding tax — graduated brackets that compute differently depending on whether your pay frequency is semi-monthly or monthly
  • Holiday pay multipliers — regular holidays, special non-working days, and rest days each have their own rates under the Labor Code
  • Overtime, late deductions, night shift differential — none of these are flat amounts
  • 13th month pay — pro-rated for employees who didn't complete the full year, with a tax exemption on the first ₱90,000

Yes, all of this can technically be done in Excel. The question is whether you can maintain accuracy across all of it as your team grows, as government rates change, and as your business gets more complex.

To put it in perspective: A single payroll run for 10 employees with mixed attendance, late deductions, and holiday pay can take 3 to 5 hours to process manually. One wrong cell reference may go unnoticed until an employee flags it, or worse, until a BIR audit surfaces it.

Where spreadsheet payroll actually breaks down

1. It has no memory of what changed

When PhilHealth adjusted to 5% in 2023, how many businesses were still running old deduction rates for the next two or three payroll cycles because nobody updated the template? That's not a skill problem. That's a process problem. Dedicated payroll software updates contribution tables automatically. A spreadsheet does not.

2. It's disconnected from attendance

Who was absent for half a day last Monday? Who worked overtime on Saturday? In a spreadsheet setup, someone has to manually encode that from a logbook, time cards, or a separate attendance sheet. Every encoding step is a chance for an error that won't surface until the employee questions their payslip.

3. There is no audit trail

Imagine an employee disputes a deduction from three months ago. You open the old file. Who edited it last? When? Which version is authoritative? In most payroll spreadsheets, you don't have a clear answer. In a payroll system, every change is logged.

4. Payslips are a separate manual step

A payslip is not just a courtesy. Employees need it for loan applications, visa requirements, and government transactions. If your payroll lives in Excel, generating individual payslips is another manual task on top of the computation itself. Another step, another hour, another place where something can go wrong.

5. It does not scale with your team

At 5 employees, the spreadsheet is manageable. At 15, it starts getting unwieldy. At 30, payroll processing is practically a part-time job. That time cost grows with your business, not against it.

The real question is not "Excel or software"

The real question is: what is your time actually worth?

If payroll takes you most of a day per cutoff, and cutoff happens twice a month, that's roughly two full days every month spent on something that could take 20 minutes. Add the stress of not being sure if the numbers are right, and you're carrying that weight every single pay period.

This is not about capability. It's about where your energy should go as a business owner. Growing your business, managing your team, and serving your customers are better uses of your time than cross-checking SSS tables every time you hire someone new.

Worth knowing: The BIR and SSS have the right to audit payroll records going back up to three years. Inaccuracies you're carrying today will not surface as a problem today. They surface later, with penalties attached.
